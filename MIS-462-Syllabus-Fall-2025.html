About the Course

Course Description

Managers need to know how to manage the diverse distributed computing environments in which they work, and leverage the opportunities these architectures provide. Integration of data and users, graphics and telephony are illustrated through emphases on client/server and N‐Tier service oriented architectures, Internet, intranet/extranet, groupware, mobile, cloud and other technologies. This elective course reviews state‐of‐the‐art technologies in each of the basic software and hardware arenas, while emphasizing management models and higher‐level analysis. Practical projects are assigned, giving students real‐world opportunities to use the tools to enhance their work and build productivity. Theory and models are taught with a management perspective as opposed to platform‐specific training. Participants are asked to complete a comprehensive and applied class project.

Prerequisites

MIS 310: Principles of Management Information Systems

Competence Statement

Knows the basic principles of distributed computing, the process of systems development and structured methodologies to accomplish these tasks, and understands the system life cycle. Can perform the functions required to accomplish these tasks at least at an introductory level.

Learning Objectives

Upon completion of this course, students will be able to:

  • Describe the concept of Information and distributed system
  • Understand the issues unique to a distributed/service oriented architecture
  • Discuss trends in distributed systems architecture
  • Explain and implement concepts of Web Services, Service Oriented Architecture
  • Explain concepts of cloud computing
  • Explain and implement principles of managing change issues while working on Modernization projects

Course Policies

Preparation and Student Effort

For a typical university course, you are expected to spend 3 to 4 hours per credit per week in your studies. In this 4-credit course, you should spend 12 to 16 hours per week completing readings, assignments, discussions, and research.

Late Assignment Policy

Assignments are expected on the date due. Exceptions can only be made by prior arrangement with the instructor. The instructor is under no obligation to award any points for assignments submitted after the due date. There will be no makeup exams or assignments offered.

Attendance Policy

Federal Financial Aid regulations require that the University track ongoing attendance. Students receiving financial aid are expected to maintain ongoing progress. Due to the participatory nature of this course, you may not earn competence if you miss more than two sessions.

Plagiarism and Copyright

Plagiarism is defined as claiming another person's work as one's own. If plagiarism is found, a one-time opportunity to redo the assignment may be provided (with up to 50% point reduction). Future plagiarized work will receive a grade of zero. Repeated plagiarism will result in course failure.
